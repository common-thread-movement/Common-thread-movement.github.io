Civic Identity and the Collapse of Empires

Empires are not held together by conquest alone; they endure because the people they conquer come to see themselves as participants in a shared civic identity that promises a new kind of belonging and a path toward individual meaning. Rome perfected this logic by turning conquered tribes—Gauls, Iberians, Greeks, Syrians—into Romans, not through blood, but through a civic nationalism that declared the empire was a project anyone could join and rise within. The Mongols discovered a parallel formula in their own way: they broke tribes, uprooted clans, and then folded Persians, Turks, Chinese, Armenians, and Kipchaks into their armies, granting them a new supra-tribal identity as members of the Great Ulus. In both systems, people who had lost their ancestral home were given an imperial home instead, and the only way to express individuality within that new home was to push its borders outward. Expansion thus became psychologically natural, because the newly conquered saw imperial growth not as servitude but as personal opportunity. A Gaul who fought for Caesar became more Roman; a Turkic horseman who rode for Möngke became more Mongol.

This is why the most cohesive eras of both empires were those in which civic identity was stronger than tribal loyalty: the frontiers were defended not simply by the center imposing its will but by the frontier peoples themselves, who believed their fate and the empire’s fate were the same. But every empire has a limit. As distance from the center increases, and as administration thins, the civic identity that once bound peoples together begins to fray. Frontier populations, no longer rewarded or included in the imperial project, revert to older loyalties—tribe, language, region, clan. The moment this shift occurs, the empire’s collapse has already begun. Rome fell when frontier soldiers no longer thought of themselves as Romans but as Goths, Franks, or Berbers. The Mongol Empire shattered when Persian, Turkic, and Mongol elites ceased to see themselves as a unified imperial people and instead revived ancestral divisions that had never fully disappeared. An empire grows when civic identity overrides tribal identity, and it dies when tribal identity resurfaces and replaces the civic one.

This dynamic is not limited to the ancient world; it can be seen today in the Middle East, where a vast mosaic of minorities—Kurds, Assyrians, Yazidis, Copts, Amazigh, Druze, Maronites, Circassians, Armenians, Turkmen—have for a century been compressed under the colonial-era civic identity of “Arab.” This identity was never truly ethnic; it was a constructed civic umbrella imposed first by European cartographers and later by post-colonial governments attempting to replicate the unity of an empire without the inclusive mechanisms that make imperial civic identity function. For a time it produced real cohesion, especially in urban centers where upward mobility required shedding local identities for a pan-Arab civic ideal. But that cohesion has eroded as the central states weakened, economic systems failed to reward inclusion, and distant provinces felt alienated from national capitals—exactly the pattern that doomed Rome and the Mongols. The resurgence of Kurdish nationalism, Assyrian identity, sectarian fragmentation, and tribal militias mirrors the imperial pattern precisely: where civic identity weakens, tribal identity returns; where tribal identity returns, fragmentation follows. The Middle East today offers the clearest modern example of how an empire-like civic nationalism—“Arabness”—can temporarily bind disparate peoples, yet inevitably collapses when the civic promise fails to provide belonging, opportunity, or a path to individual meaning. Whether in Rome, in the Mongol steppe, or in the modern Arab world, the political center holds only as long as the frontier believes its future is intertwined with the imperial project. When that belief dies, the empire dissolves, not through invasion, but through the quiet and unstoppable reawakening of older loyalties.
